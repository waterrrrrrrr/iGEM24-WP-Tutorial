Orthogonality Verification Experiment Design

Orthogonality: Compatibility between hairpin DNAs corresponding to different targets → Co-incubation to observe the operation of the system

RNA: Observed using gel electrophoresis

*Marker: Mark the positions corresponding to different base pairs (bp); H1: Hairpin DNA responsive to the target; H1’: Hairpin responsive to other targets; H2/H3: HCR hairpin DNA responsive to the target; H2’/H3’: Other HCR hairpins; pH: "+" indicates alkaline conditions, "-" indicates acidic conditions

In the table, +/- indicates whether the substance is added. The occurrence of HCR is determined by comparing band positions: HCR response generates high molecular weight bands.

Taking a single target for a single test as an example, lanes 2-5 confirm the positions of the target, H1, H2, H3 respectively.

In theory, HCR response bands should only appear when the target, H1, H2, H3, and alkaline pH conditions are present, but pH response leaks cannot be ruled out.

Orthogonality testing is completed by adding other targets and their H2/H3. Band positions and intensities are observed to determine:

1. Whether there are abnormal HCR activations under conditions not meeting the above conditions.

2. Whether lane 12 shows unexpected activation of H1 relative to lane 10.

Target Capture Sensitivity Experiment Design

Sensitivity Verification For MiRNA Binding:

Different concentrations of target RNA molecules are added with constant quantity of hairpin DNA for HCR and the probe hairpin under activated and inactive condition, and the size of HCR products will change according to the amount of targets. By comparing the concentration of targets when HCR starts and the size and shade of HCR bands, we can confirm how the reaction carries out and determine the sensitivity of target capture. Probe stability can be increased by adjusting the lengths of stem and toe domains.

The detection limit of target binding is determined by combining gel electrophoresis analysis and LFIA (Lateral Flow Immunoassay).

Electricity Generation Level Experiment Design - HCR Parameters & Array Testing

We utilize methylene blue (MB) as the electroactive substance.

The principle involves pipetting a mixture of MB, samples, and hairpin DNA onto a gold electrode and incubating as required (changing concentrations, pH, and temperature conditions in HCR parameter determination), followed by measuring the intensity of electricity.

This setup allows for the investigation of the effects of various parameters on the electricity generation level, providing insights into the optimization of the HCR process for enhanced electrical output.

We use an electrochemical workstation as the electrochemical detection instrument.

The experimental process involves comparing the electrical intensity of the modified electrode before and after incubation with the mixture (testing electrodes with different reaction durations in HCR parameter determination). This process allows us to determine the electricity generation level of our designed method, and the parameters obtained can be used for modeling references.

Biosafety Experiment Design

Risk of Nucleic Acid Leakage

We will utilize environmental DNA detection technology to assess the nucleic acid leakage of our product under various usage conditions, thereby evaluating associated risks.

Multiple Scenarios:

  • Storage in a 4°C refrigerator
  • Brief placement at room temperature (20°C)
  • Long-term storage at room temperature

eDNA Detection Process:

  • Storage in a 4°C refrigerator
  • Brief placement at room temperature (20°C)
  • Long-term storage at room temperature

Handling Liquid Hazards and Recovery

1. Hazard Validation

Considering the storage and usage scenarios of our product, we will assess the hazards of the handling liquid from three aspects: food, water quality, and skin contact.

For hazard validation related to food and skin contact (mouse experiments), a simple control experiment design will be used. For water quality hazard experiments, a gradient experiment method will be employed, with water quality parameters (pH, dissolved oxygen, conductivity, turbidity, temperature) serving as monitoring indicators.

2. Recovery and Disposal

Considering existing nucleic acid waste liquid treatment methods, when the volume of waste liquid is low, traditional filtration and separation methods will be employed, followed by chemical precipitation and solid-liquid separation treatment（Coagulation-SBR）. When the volume of waste liquid is high, it will be handed over to waste liquid treatment companies or instrument disposal services.
